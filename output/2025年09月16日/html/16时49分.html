【Deal?: US, China Reach TikTok ‘Consensus’ in Spain, Maybe Trump-Xi Call】US-China talks were <b>“respectful, wide-ranging and in-depth,”</b> Bessent said during a briefing on Monday outside the Santa Cruz Palace, in the center of Madrid, where the talks took place. The two sides talked for <b>six hours</b> or so on Monday after going late into the night the previous day. There were no major developments in trade discussions, however, with <b>Greer saying talks on “those kinds of things” had been “deferred” to another time</b>. <br/><b>US </b><br/>- <b>President Donald Trump said he would speak with Chinese leader Xi Jinping on Friday</b>. He hinted that he’ll pursue a wider trade accord in contacts with Xi. Trump said his top concern was “frankly, more importantly, a big deal.” <br/>- US Treasury Secretary <b>Scott Bessent</b> told reporters that a framework to keep ByteDance Ltd.’s TikTok app running in the US had been reached.​ “I think the framework is for it to switch to US-controlled ownership.” Bessent told reporters Monday, “<b>We will be holding trade negotiations in about a month, again at a different location</b>.” The Treasury chief said it “remains to be seen” whether a trade deal can be done with China before the APEC summit. Bessent said a September 17 deadline that could have disrupted the popular social media app in the U.S. encouraged Chinese negotiators to reach a potential deal. He said that <b>deadline could be extended by 90 days to allow the deal to be finalized</b>, but declined to discuss specifics of the deal. Bessent said when commercial terms of the deal are revealed, it will preserve cultural aspects of TikTok that Chinese negotiators care about.<br/>- US Trade Representative <b>Jamieson Greer</b> indicated that another extension in the pause on the highest tariff levels is possible when the November deadline arrives.<br/>- The Madrid talks were focused on TikTok, with negotiations on other topics essentially deferred, Bessent and Greer indicated.<br/>- <b>A White House official</b> said if the leaders of both nations agree, <b>the terms would be similar to a proposal the U.S. reviewed in April</b>. Under that proposal, a consortium of investors would take an ownership stake in TikTok. <b>Private-equity firm Blackstone</b>, which was previously a contender to be part of the consortium, <b>is no longer part of the potential deal, according to people familiar with the matter.</b> The White House created an official TikTok account last month. Trump’s attempt to save the platform is a reversal from his first term, when he sought to ban it, then blessed a tentative agreement to save it in which Oracle and Walmart would have invested. The deal never went through. Co-founded by Republican megadonor Larry Ellison, the world’s second-richest person, <b>Oracle </b>hosts TikTok user data and is expected to be involved in the new agreement.<br/><b>China</b><br/>- China’s Vice Commerce Minister Li Chenggang told reporters in Madrid that a framework of “consensus” had been reached on TikTok, while cautioning that Beijing won’t sacrifice principles for a deal. China opposes the politicization and weaponization of technology and trade matters and will safeguard its national interests and the rights and interests of its companies, Li said.<br/>- Wang Jingtao, a deputy director of China’s Cyberspace Administration, referred to methods such as the entrusted operation of TikTok’s US user data, and mentioned algorithms and intellectual property rights, without getting into specifics.<br/><b>Ahead of the Talk</b><br/>- Last week, Secretary of State Marco Rubio and Secretary of Defense Pete Hegseth spoke with their Chinese counterparts. Had there been no deal on TikTok, a Trump-Xi meeting on the sidelines of the Asia Pacific Economic Cooperation gathering in South Korea late next month would have been off the table, the official said.<br/>- Ahead of the talks on Sunday, China launched two investigations targeting the US semiconductor industry.<br/>- On Monday, China’s market regulator said that a preliminary investigation found Nvidia was in violation of the country’s anti-competition laws, adding that a further probe into the U.S. chip giant will be carried out.
